Now we reach the core evaluative mindset. The challenge YAML gives a trust, verify, reject framework, and the chapter expands that into a full critical evaluation model.

[JAMIE]

[ALEX]
For every Copilot suggestion, ask: is it factually accurate? Is it clearer than the original? Does it match the document’s tone? Does it introduce accessibility issues? Did it add content you didn’t ask for?

[JAMIE]

[ALEX]

[JAMIE]
So when should you trust Copilot?

[ALEX]
More readily for low-risk, easily verifiable tasks: grammar cleanup, rough brainstorming, formatting suggestions, possible headings, draft rewrites you can inspect line by line.

[JAMIE]
When should you verify?

[ALEX]
Almost always for substantive claims, instructions, examples, links, accessibility advice, configuration details, commands, policy statements, and anything user-facing.

[JAMIE]
And when should you reject?

[ALEX]
Reject when it is factually wrong, adds invented links, becomes less clear, changes the tone in an unhelpful way, introduces accessibility regressions, misrepresents the audience, or performs a task other than the one you requested.

[JAMIE]
What are the common failure modes the chapter warns about?

[ALEX]
Hallucinated links. Over-complicated language. Lost context. Accessibility regressions. Also overconfidence, hidden assumptions, and suggestions that appear polished but are not grounded in the repository or the workshop’s goals.

[JAMIE]
Can you say more about accessibility-specific concerns?

[ALEX]
Certainly. AI may produce alt text that is too vague or too long. It may suggest tables where lists would be simpler. It may remove semantic structure. It may use visual wording without considering screen reader users. It may recommend decorative images be described when empty alt text would be more appropriate, or vice versa. Accessibility requires judgment about purpose, not just wording.

[JAMIE]
So “sounds descriptive” is not enough.

[ALEX]
Exactly. You need to ask what the content is doing.

[JAMIE]
What’s the right mental model, then?

[ALEX]
Copilot is a fast drafting partner. It is not a source of truth, not an accessibility expert, not a repository maintainer, and not your editor-in-chief. You stay responsible for the final content.
